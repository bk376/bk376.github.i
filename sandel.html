The Case Against Perfection

  1. Home
  2. blog

the quest for mastery
10 Dec 2015 12 Comment enhancement, perfection, genetic engineering

Michael Sandel’s book, The Case Against Perfection: Ethics in the Age of Genetic Engineering, is a critique of the ambitions of parents, scientists, and societies who would employ genetic enhancement in the pursuit of improving human physical and mental capacities. Sandel admits that enhancement can be beneficial in improving human lives but worries that seeking perfection can be dangerous. The main problem with enhancement and genetic engineering according to Sandel is that they represent a kind of “hyper-agency”. He is worried that if we try to control and change our nature through enhancement, we may not be able to foresee some of the negative effects of the changes (Sandel 26). Buchanan takes a different stand in the article, Why Cognitive Enhancement Is Your Future (and Your Past). The only way for human beings to survive, according to Buchanan, is to cognitively enhance their flawed physical and memory capacities. In support of cognitive enhancement, he claims that striving to improve human capacities is very much part of human nature and this has been achieved in the past through literacy, numeracy, and institutions of science (Andersen 3). While Sandel believes that it is part of human nature to have flawed capacities and thus enhancing them undermines human dignity, Buchana insists that striving for enhancement is part of human nature and is essential to ensure survival. The two philosophers disagree about the consequences of cognitive enhancement on human life because of these different views on human nature and dignity.

Although Buchanan and Sandel disagree in most of their views on genetic enhancement, they acknowledge that it can be beneficial in improving human lives. Sandel suggests that genetic enhancement might be our hope towards achieving our dream of finding cure and prevention for ailments like cancer. He asserts that, “breakthroughs in genetics present us with a promise that we may soon be able to treat and prevent a host of debilitating diseases” (Sandel 5). Genetic diseases like Cystic Fibrosis and Huntington’s disease are caused by small differences in the DNA we get from each of our parents. Research in the past has shown that these diseases might be cured through genetic engineering. Buchanan also believes that cognitive enhancement expands human physical capacities and comes with great advantages like overcoming our traditional limitations in memory. He insists that human beings have flawed capacities and cognitive enhancement is essential to ensure survival (Andersen 10). But while Buchanan is very optimistic about cognitive enhancement, Sandel is wary of overusing it.

While Sandel admits that cognitive enhancement can be beneficial, he is concerned that it would diminish human values like humility. He is worried that as human beings manage to control their lives and themselves through cognitive enhancement, the more their self-confidence and arrogance grows and this easily leads to hubris. He says, “The social basis of humility will be diminished if people became accustomed to genetic self-improvement. The awareness that our talents and abilities are not wholly our own doing restrains our tendency toward hubris” (Sandel 86). Appreciating our flawed nature is a demonstration of humility and trying to remake it destroys this moral value. If genetic engineering enables human beings to override the results of the genetic lottery, Sandel insists that advantaged individuals will more likely regard themselves as self-made and would take too much credit for their own talents and achievements (Sandel 91). The “natural talents” that enable the successful to flourish are gifts that should be shared with those who lack them through no fault of their own. Attributing these achievements to themselves [the successful] will thus likely change this notion of sharing talents. Consequently, the less fortunate in the society would be viewed as unfit. Buchanan underscores this notion of degradation of humility. Although he agrees that hubris creates social problems, he suggests that it should not be the reason to object to cognitive enhancement. He argues that enhancements have always been a big part of how human beings have survived and succeeded throughout our entire history (Andersen 10).

Besides the lack of humility that the quest for perfection cultivates, Sandel is concerned that striving for mastery can cause other negative social consequences. While he admits that there have always been inequalities among human beings, he is concerned about those who might decide not to use genetic enhancement and the disadvantaged who might not have access to cognitive enhancements. He points that the use of cognitive enhancement threatens to create two classes of human beings out of those who use enhancements and others who would prefer not to (Sandel 15). Theses classes will consist of individuals with superhuman qualities on one hand and “weak” beings on the other and this division in the society would threaten the social relationship among human beings. But while Sandel points to this negative social consequence, Buchanan insists that social inequalities that could result by disproportionately advantaging elites can be settled if enhancement drugs are made available to everyone (Andersen 7). He is optimistic that social inequality would only be short-lived for the period when enhancement drugs would not be accessible but eventually will diminish. He argues, “in the future there might be a period when these drugs are on patent, and are expensive, but when they go off patent they could become very inexpensive” (Andersen 7). He believes that negative social consequences would not be a problem as long as the enhancements become available to all. He adds that films like Gattaca mislead by taking very negative view of the social consequences of genetic engineering leaving out the benefits. However, his argument misses Sandel’s deeper concern for those who would prefer not to use enhancements.

The disagreements between Buchanan and Sandel are centered on their contrasting understanding of human nature. While Sandel believes that flawed physical and mental capacities are part of what makes us human, Buchanan claims that cognitively enhancing those flawed capacities is essential to ensure survival. Sandel argues that not only do enhancements undermine human dignity by creating a robotic nature of human abilities, but also striving for mastery threaten to diminish the appreciation of the talents we already possess. People become concerned with perfecting their abilities at the expense of neglecting the natural gifts they have. According to him, we should instead celebrate our nature the way it is. He says, “Rather than employ our new genetic powers to straighten ‘the crooked timber of humanity,’ we should...make arrangements more hospitable to the gifts and limitations of ‘imperfect’ human beings” (Sandel 97). He wants us to celebrate this flawed human nature. He believes that we were created the way we are and we should appreciate our human nature as it is. Buchanan’s stance is based on his assumption that striving for enhancement is part of human nature and insists that enhancement has been achieved in the past through literacy and education. Buchanan thus supports cognitive enhancement because he thinks evolution is inevitable while Sandel believes that we are perfect with our flawed capacities.

While there are reasons to support genetic enhancement, it can come with dire consequences that are worth considering. Naturally, human beings are concerned with improving what they have even if it means sacrificing a few lives to achieve something that will benefit everyone else. While genetic enhancement would be of great benefit to human beings but striving for mastery does not promise anything good. We should therefore support striving for enhancement as opposed to striving for perfection.

Should human beings be genetically enhanced?
